Title: The Effects of Aerobic Exercise on Glucose Regulation and Insulin Sensitivity in a Mouse Model of Diabetes Mellitus. Abstract:Diabetes Mellitus is a chronic metabolic disorder characterized by hyperglycemia due to impaired glucose regulation and insulin sensitivity. Regular physical activity, such as aerobic exercise, has been widely recognized as an effective non-pharmacological intervention for improving glucose control and insulin sensitivity in diabetic individuals. However, the underlying mechanisms by which aerobic exercise exerts its beneficial effects on glucose metabolism remain unclear. In this experimental study, we aimed to investigate the effects of aerobic exercise on glucose regulation and insulin sensitivity in a mouse model of Diabetes Mellitus. Male C57BL/6 mice were divided into two groups: control group (n=10) and diabetic group (n=10). Diabetes was induced by a single intraperitoneal injection of streptozotocin. The mice in the exercise group were subjected to a treadmill exercise program consisting of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise for 40 minutes, five days per week, for a duration of eight weeks. The control group remained sedentary throughout the study period.  At the end of the eight-week intervention period, fasting blood glucose levels, insulin levels, and glucose tolerance were assessed using standardized laboratory techniques. Insulin sensitivity was evaluated through the homeostatic model assessment-insulin resistance index (HOMA-IR). Muscle tissue samples were collected from both groups to measure key indicators of glucose metabolism, including glucose transporter type 4 (GLUT4) protein expression and glycogen content. Preliminary results indicated a significant decrease in fasting blood glucose levels in the exercise group compared to the sedentary diabetic group (p < 0.05). Furthermore, the exercise group exhibited improved glucose tolerance, as evidenced by a lower area under the curve during an oral glucose tolerance test (p < 0.01). Insulin levels were also reduced in the exercise group compared to diabetic sedentary mice (p < 0.05). Notably, HOMA-IR values were significantly lower in the exercise group, indicating enhanced insulin sensitivity (p < 0.01). Immunohistochemical analysis revealed increased GLUT4 protein expression in skeletal muscle tissue of exercise group compared to controls (p < 0.01), suggesting improved glucose transport into muscle cells. Additionally, glycogen content in skeletal muscle was significantly higher in the exercise group, suggesting enhanced glucose storage capacity (p < 0.05). In conclusion, our findings demonstrate that aerobic exercise in a mouse model of Diabetes Mellitus leads to improved glucose regulation, enhanced insulin sensitivity, and increased GLUT4 expression in skeletal muscle. These results highlight the potential therapeutic effects of aerobic exercise as a non-pharmacological strategy for managing Diabetes Mellitus. Further investigations are warranted to elucidate the specific molecular pathways involved in these exercise-induced improvements in glucose metabolism and insulin sensitivity.

Streptozotocin-induced diabetic rats were orally administered aqueous extracts of the aforementioned herbs for a duration of 4 weeks. Blood glucose levels were monitored regularly, and additional parameters such as body weight, lipid profile, and pancreatic histopathology were also assessed. The results demonstrated a significant reduction in fasting blood glucose levels in the herbal extract-treated groups compared to the control diabetic group (p<0.05). Additionally, improved oral glucose tolerance was observed in the treated groups, indicating enhanced glucose metabolism. The treated animals also exhibited a restoration in body weight and improvement in the dyslipidemic profile, including reduced serum triglycerides, total cholesterol, and low-density lipoprotein (LDL) levels, and increased high-density lipoprotein (HDL) levels. Histopathological analysis of the pancreatic tissue revealed protected β-cells in the treated groups, indicating a potential for preserving and enhancing insulin production. Furthermore, the herbal extracts displayed notable antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects, which may have contributed to their therapeutic mechanisms against diabetes. In conclusion, this experimental study provides important evidence on the therapeutic potential of Momordica charantia, Gymnema sylvestre, and Trigonella foenum-graecum extracts in the management of diabetes mellitus. The observed anti-diabetic effects, including glucose-lowering properties, improved lipid profile, and protection to pancreatic β-cells, highlight the promising role of these herbal extracts as potential adjunctive therapies for DM. Further investigations are warranted to elucidate the underlying mechanisms and determine the optimal dosage and treatment regimen to maximize their therapeutic benefits.

Title: Investigating the Effect of Antioxidant Therapy on Glycemic Control and Oxidative Stress Parameters in an Experimental Model of Diabetes Mellitus. Abstract:Diabetes mellitus is a chronic metabolic disorder characterized by hyperglycemia resulting from defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both. Oxidative stress, which occurs due to an imbalance between the production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and antioxidant defenses, plays a significant role in the pathogenesis and progression of diabetes mellitus. This study aims to investigate the effect of antioxidant therapy on glycemic control and oxidative stress parameters in an experimental model of diabetes mellitus. A total of 50 male Wistar rats were divided into five groups: control, diabetes mellitus (DM), DM + antioxidant therapy (DM+AT), antioxidant therapy alone (AT), and DM + placebo. Diabetes mellitus was induced by a single intraperitoneal injection of streptozotocin. The AT group received a daily dose of an antioxidant formulation orally for a period of six weeks. After the experimental period, fasting blood glucose levels, oral glucose tolerance, and glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) levels were assessed in all groups. Additionally, various oxidative stress parameters such as malondialdehyde (MDA) levels, catalase (CAT) activity, and total antioxidant capacity (TAC) were measured. Results showed that the DM group exhibited significantly higher fasting blood glucose levels, impaired glucose tolerance, and increased HbA1c levels compared to the control group (p < 0.05). Conversely, the DM+AT group demonstrated improved glycemic control, indicated by significantly lower fasting blood glucose levels and improved oral glucose tolerance (p < 0.05). Furthermore, the DM+AT group exhibited a significant decrease in MDA levels and a significant increase in CAT activity and TAC compared to the DM group (p < 0.05). Similar changes in oxidative stress parameters were not observed in the AT group, indicating that the observed effects were specific to the diabetic condition. These findings suggest that antioxidant therapy has potential implications in the management of diabetes mellitus, as it improves glycemic control and attenuates oxidative stress. The reduction in oxidative stress markers observed in the DM+AT group suggests that antioxidants play a crucial role in minimizing the deleterious effects of ROS, which are implicated in the development of insulin resistance and diabetic complications. Further studies are warranted to elucidate the underlying mechanisms and potential clinical applications, paving the way for novel therapeutic strategies to combat diabetes mellitus and its associated complications.
Title: The Effects of Vitamin D Supplementation on Glycemic Control in Experimental Diabetes Mellitus: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis.. Abstract:Introduction: Diabetes mellitus (DM) is a chronic metabolic disorder characterized by impaired insulin function and subsequent hyperglycemia. Numerous studies have suggested a potential link between vitamin D deficiency and the development of DM. This systematic review and meta-analysis aims to evaluate the effects of vitamin D supplementation on glycemic control in experimental models of DM. Methods: A comprehensive literature search was conducted using electronic databases. Animal studies that investigated the impact of vitamin D supplementation on glycemic control in experimental models of DM were included. Data were extracted and analyzed using standardized procedures for meta-analysis. Results: The initial search retrieved 236 articles, of which 15 met the inclusion criteria. The meta-analysis included a total of 745 animals. Pooled analysis demonstrated a significant improvement in glycemic control in the vitamin D group compared to the control group (standardized mean difference: -1.15; 95% confidence interval: -1.61 to -0.68). Subgroup analysis revealed that vitamin D supplementation led to favorable glycemic outcomes in both type 1 and type 2 DM models. Conclusion: This systematic review and meta-analysis strongly support the potential beneficial effects of vitamin D supplementation on glycemic control in experimental models of DM. These findings suggest that vitamin D may have a therapeutic role in the management of DM by improving glucose homeostasis. Further research, including randomized controlled trials, is warranted to determine the exact mechanisms by which vitamin D exerts its antidiabetic effects and to translate these findings into clinical practice.

These interventions include immunomodulatory agents, such as monoclonal antibodies targeting T cells or cytokines involved in the DMT1 pathogenesis, as well as immune cell therapies aimed at reprogramming autoreactive T cells or inducing immune tolerance. In conclusion, DMT1 is a multifaceted disease characterized by immune-mediated destruction of pancreatic beta cells. Understanding the complex immunological pathways involved in its pathogenesis is crucial for the development of targeted therapeutic interventions. Combining glycemic control with immunomodulatory approaches holds promise for halting or even reversing the progression of DMT1, ultimately improving the quality of life for individuals living with this chronic condition.
